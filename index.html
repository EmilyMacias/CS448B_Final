Breaking Down Investment

Investing is a key part of financial success, being the differentiating factor from holding onto our money and having it lose value versus having it grow as inflation increases over the years. So why don't more people do it? Who is empowered to invest early on in their lives and who is not?


There are groups who are hisorically less likely to invest than others with many contibuting factors like access to financial education, experience with generational wealth, access to extra income, and even gender taking a driving role. However, access to financial education and the fair opportunity to build wealth is a key right, setting the stage for better access to education, ability to support a family, and through studies published in the NIH even better mental health outcomes.


Based on this, it is key to empower everyone to invest and build fruitful money habits. To begin, let's explore what a small snippet of the investment landscape looks like in terms of who is currently empowered to invest. The visualization below pulls from the 2024 National Financial Capability Study (NFCS) and shows only a small snapshot of the population in terms of gender, age, income, and education. While not fully representative of the investing population in the US, it gives us a small window into the financial lives of 25,000 US adults. What do you notice?

































We can see that age, income, and education are all factors that contribute to the likelihood of investing. Gender has also been referenced as a major factor in the likelihood of investing, with women investing less than men. However, this is not represented in the current data. This can be for a varity of reasons, including this gender gap slowly closing as more women are empowered to invest or alternatively, due to the data not being fully representative of the investing population in the US. We see similar surprises in other categories like for income where individuals with lower incomes are seemingly more willing to try and invest. We can theorize about why this is the case, from attempts to use investing as a way to build wealth more quickly or drastically to potentially trying to ensure a stable plan b not provided by employer benefits (e.g. 401k). Regardless, to fill the gap for those that do not feel empowered to invest and to ensure that those that do invest have a good understanding of the basics, let's explore two basic and key ways to invest via visualizations: stocks and real estate.




Financial Literacy Resources

Now that we have briefly explored the investment landscape, let's explore what it looks like to invest properly and why it can be so powerful. To do this we will aim to understand three main topics: What generational wealth is and how investments contribute, how we can invest safely, and what different types of investments can be and return.


Let's start at the beginning, what even is an investment? Investments can take many different forms, from traditional stock holdings to real estate investments. Stock holdings can include purchasing of individual company stocks (e.g. purchasing individual Nvidia stocks) or purchasing a set of stocks which are composed of many different individual comapny stocks (e.g. S&P 500 index funds which are composed of 500 different stocks). Individual stocks can lead to larger returns if an individual company is doing very well, but are more risky because of something called volatility. Volatility refers to how much the stock can fluctuate over time. For example, an individual company stock can rise in price but then also crash in price very quickly. On the other hand, a set of aggregated individual stocks like in the S&P 500 has lower volatility because fluctutations in the individual stocks often balance out.


Alternatively, a common way to earn interest on your money is via a HYSA (High Yield Savings Accounts) which is not an investment but rather a savings account with a high interest rate. Unlike investments in the stock market, you cannot lose money because the interest rate will never go below zero, however, the trade-off is that this additional safety comes with a much lower reward than is possible with stock investments.


A unifying important factor among all of thes options is the concept of timing. Based on how long you keep your investment and when you decide to buy or sell, the returns can be drastically different.To better understand how investment works in terms of losses, gains, and time, let's explore what our returns would have looked like if we had invested in the S&P 500 at different points in time versus if we had invested in a HYSA account or not invested at all. Pay close attention to how returns relate to the amount of time the money was invested. What do you notice? (Note: we will assume for the HYSA that the interest rate is 4.2% annually)

Real Estate Investments

Now that we have gotten the chance to explore investment through avenues like HYSA accounts and the S&P 500, let's understand another key avenue for investment: real estate. While real estate is an investment just like stocks, it is unique because it is also closely associated with generational wealth and is passed down through generations. Let's explore how real estate can be an investment more interactively.


Choose a home to buy and sell it when you see fit, how much money do you make? How much do you lose? Try to think of parents, grandparents, and great-grandparents buying and selling homes, how does it help them build wealth? How would it help you build wealth by inheriting it?

We can see from our exploration that just like traditional investments through stocks, real estate investments also increase if held for longer periods of time. Similarly, they can also cause losses if held for a shorter period of time when the housing market is not doing well. Additionally, a unique point with real estate is that since it is an asset that is passed down to children and gradchildren, the profits are even more powerful if homes are fully paid off since not just the profit, but the full value of the home is given to the beneficiary who sells it. Lastly, real estate is a key example of how impactful and long lasting generational wealth can be. Notice how the more expensive homes had a higher profit over time.
